Practice 3: Integration and Study of Dynamics in Gazebo and ROS2

Objective

The main objective of this lab is to correctly configure and simulate a mobile manipulator robot in ROS 2 using a model created in Blender. The robot must include key sensors like an IMU and cameras, and be fully compatible with ROS 2 standards (REP-103 and REP-105). It should be operable via RViz and teleoperation tools, enabling manipulation tasks like picking and placing an object. Finally, motion data is recorded for power analysis and visualized through plots.

ROSBAG

During the teleoperation and motion execution phases, all relevant data from the robot's sensors and joints were recorded using rosbag. This data includes joint efforts and IMU measurements, which are essential for analyzing the power consumption of the pick and place mechanism.

Results Analysis

Plot 1: Time vs Wheel Position

This plot shows the angular position of each wheel over time during the teleoperation. An initial steady increase is seen across all wheels until they reach a maximum of around 2.2 radians, indicating a straight and controlled motion. The alignment of the curves confirms synchronized movement among the wheels.

Plot 2: Time vs Wheel Velocity (Acceleration)

This plot illustrates the changes in rotational velocity of the wheels over time. An initial spike represents the robot’s start-up motion, followed by a few brief fluctuations, likely from minor adjustments during teleoperation. The velocity then stabilizes near zero, indicating that the robot reached its destination and came to a stop.

These two plots are crucial for understanding the mobile platform’s dynamic behavior and validating that the recorded data aligns with the planned trajectory and actions.

Plot 3: Time vs G-parcial (Joint Power Consumption)

This plot represents the partial power consumption (G-parcial) of each joint involved in the pick and place mechanism over time. The G-parcial is computed as the sum of torque efforts applied to the joints during motion execution, especially from the moment the arm starts the pick action until it returns to its initial position.

We expect the following behaviors in this plot:

  • At the beginning (before the pick), the G-parcial values should be close to zero, as the arm is idle.
  • During the picking phase, a sharp increase in effort values is expected, as the joints apply torque to reach and grasp the object.
  • While holding and moving the object, G-parcial values may remain elevated depending on the weight distribution.
  • As the object is placed on the robot and the arm returns to its home position, a decrease in effort is expected, eventually stabilizing again near zero.

This plot is essential to identify which joints contribute the most to power consumption and to optimize the motion planning and mechanical design for energy efficiency.
